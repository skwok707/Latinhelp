Declining Nouns

Nouns in Latin have variable endings based on their function. These functions are displayed by 'cases' which can be identified by a variation in the noun's endings. Latin has five major declensions, each with unique sets of endings for different cases. The primary cases in Latin are Nominative, Genitive, Dative, Accusative, Ablative, and Vocative. Let's go through each declension with their respective endings in singular and plural forms.

First Declension (mostly feminine)

Case Singular Plural

Nominative -a -ae

Genitive -ae -ārum

Dative -ae -īs

Accusative -am -ās

Ablative -ā -īs

Vocative -a -ae

Example: puella (girl)

Singular: puella, puellae, puellae, puellam, puellā, puella

Plural: puellae, puellārum, puellīs, puellās, puellīs, puellae
